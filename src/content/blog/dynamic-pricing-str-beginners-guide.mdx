If you're still setting a flat nightly rate for your short-term rental, you're almost certainly leaving money on the table — sometimes 20–30% of potential revenue.

Dynamic pricing is the practice of adjusting your nightly rate based on real-time demand signals: local events, seasonal patterns, day-of-week behavior, competitor availability, and more. Hotels have used it for decades. STR hosts are catching up.

## What Is Dynamic Pricing?

Dynamic pricing means your rate changes — often daily — to reflect actual market conditions. A Saturday night during a local festival commands a premium. A Tuesday in January in a beach town might need a discount to avoid sitting vacant.

The goal is never to just raise prices. It's to price each night at the exact rate the market will bear, minimizing empty calendar days while maximizing revenue on high-demand nights.

## Why Static Rates Fail

A fixed nightly rate is essentially a guess. It's probably right for some nights and wrong for most. Here's what that looks like in practice:

- **High-demand nights**: Your $200/night rate fills instantly while comparable properties charge $340. You've left $140 per night on the table.
- **Low-demand nights**: Your $200 rate scares off budget travelers who might have booked at $140. You sit vacant.

Multiply either scenario across a full year and you can see why revenue management matters.

## The Three Approaches

### 1. Static Pricing (the default)

Set it and forget it. Works for hosts with one property who aren't optimizing for income. You'll be competitive some nights, underpriced others, and overpriced more than you realize.

### 2. Pricing Tools (DIY)

Platforms like PriceLabs, Wheelhouse, and Beyond Pricing connect to your listing and automatically adjust rates using their algorithms. They're affordable ($20–$100/month) and better than doing nothing.

The limitation: algorithms set prices, but strategy is still on you. Understanding minimum stays, orphan nights, seasonality curves, and market-specific nuances requires expertise most hosts don't have time to develop.

### 3. Managed Revenue Services

A revenue management firm handles pricing strategy end-to-end — tool selection, configuration, ongoing adjustments, and strategic decisions like when to extend minimum stays or run promotions.

## How to Think About Occupancy vs. Rate

A common misconception: high occupancy = good performance. Not necessarily.

A property with 95% occupancy at $150/night generates $52,125 annually. The same property at 78% occupancy and $185/night generates $52,636 — more revenue with fewer guest turnover costs.

The metric to watch is **RevPAR** (Revenue Per Available Room/Night). It accounts for both rate and occupancy.

## What Signals Drive Dynamic Pricing

Good pricing algorithms — and good human strategists — look at:

- **Competitor rates and availability**: What are comparable properties charging tonight vs. next weekend?
- **Local events**: Concerts, festivals, graduations, and sporting events spike demand temporarily
- **Day-of-week patterns**: Weekends typically outperform weekdays in leisure markets
- **Booking lead time**: Last-minute bookings often warrant discounts; far-out bookings may support premiums
- **Seasonal curves**: Every market has a unique seasonality profile
- **Your own booking pace**: How fast are nights filling relative to prior years?

## The Minimum Stay Strategy Most Hosts Miss

Pricing is only half the equation. Minimum stay rules profoundly affect revenue.

A 2-night minimum blocks a Saturday that could have been booked as a 4-night stay. A 1-night minimum lets weekend warriors fill your calendar but creates "orphan" weeknights no one wants.

Sophisticated revenue management adjusts minimum stays dynamically — shorter windows close to the date to capture last-minute demand, longer minimums further out to protect high-value weekends.

## Getting Started

If you're ready to move beyond static pricing:

1. **Audit your current performance**: Pull your last 12 months of occupancy, average nightly rate, and RevPAR
2. **Benchmark against comparable properties**: Most pricing tools offer market data; use it
3. **Set a baseline with a tool**: PriceLabs is generally the most flexible for hosts who want control
4. **Consider management if you have multiple properties**: The complexity compounds fast
